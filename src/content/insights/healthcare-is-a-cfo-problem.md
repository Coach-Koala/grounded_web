Ask a CFO about their cloud spend and you'll get a number, a trend, and a
plan. Ask about healthcare spend — frequently a bigger line — and you'll
usually get a referral to HR.

Ask HR, and you'll get a referral back to Finance.

This is the accountability gap, and it shows up in almost every conversation
we have with self-funded employers. Healthcare hides inside a "salaries and
benefits" line item, so it never gets treated as a manageable variable. The
renewal arrives, the increase gets absorbed, and everyone moves on.

Founders and finance leaders describe the same learned helplessness: _the
cost of doing business is just high, there's nothing I can do, I'm captive._

Except the numbers say otherwise. Employers routinely overpay 20–40% on
plans designed from actuarial averages and delivered through layers of
intermediaries — and a self-funded employer has both the data rights and the
contractual standing to do something about it.

The reframe that works: stop talking about benefits and start talking about
**EBITDA**. Recovered overpayments, corrected contract terms, and vendors
held to their promises don't show up in an engagement report — they show up
in operating margin.

Three questions that close the accountability gap:

1. **Who, by name, owns healthcare spend?** Not the plan — the spend.
2. **When did anyone last review paid claims** for errors and contract
   compliance, the way you'd audit any other major vendor?
3. **What did each point solution actually return** last year, in dollars?

If any answer is "I'd have to check," that's the leak.
