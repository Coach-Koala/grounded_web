title: "You own your claims data. Are you using it?"
date: "2026-07-16"
summary: "Self-funded employers have a legal right to their own claims data. Almost none of them exercise it — and the money leaks accordingly."
---

Roughly two-thirds of covered workers in the US are in self-funded health
plans — and at firms with 200 or more employees, it's the large majority
(KFF Employer Health Benefits Survey).

If that's you, here is the part nobody tells you: **that claims data is
yours.** Federal transparency rules — the Transparency in Coverage rule and
the Consolidated Appropriations Act of 2021 — tore down most of the legal
walls between you and the data your plan generates. The CAA even bans the
gag clauses that TPAs used to write into contracts to keep you from seeing
your own prices.

So why does almost nobody look?

Because the system is built around not looking. Benefits leaders we talk to
describe the same experience: carrier reporting that arrives months late,
dashboards that summarize instead of reveal, and a stack of intermediaries
who hold the detail. One called it an information desert. Meanwhile
healthcare is often a top-three operating cost — managed with less scrutiny
than the office snack budget.

What looking actually surfaces:

- **Paid billing errors.** Duplicates, unbundled charges, upcoding — errors
  that were paid because catching them was nobody's job.
- **Contract terms working against you.** Language agreed to years ago,
  priced out of line with peers, renewed by default.
- **Vendors that report activity instead of outcomes.** Engagement counts
  are not savings.

The transparency laws created the right to look. They didn't create the
habit — or the tooling. That's the gap.
